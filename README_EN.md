# ChurnGuard — Customer Churn Prediction

Final project for the Data Analytics Bootcamp. The goal was to build a system capable of predicting which customers of a telecommunications company are most likely to churn, and to do it in a way that makes real business sense, not just technical sense.

---

## The Problem

The IBM Telco dataset has a churn rate of 26.5%, meaning roughly 1 in 4 customers leaves. The challenge is not just predicting churn, but predicting it well while accounting for the actual cost of being wrong.

Losing a customer who could have been retained costs around 200 euros. Making an unnecessary retention call costs 5 euros. That is a 40 to 1 ratio, and that number changes everything when it comes to model decision-making.

---

## The Data

The project uses the public IBM Telco Customer Churn dataset, which contains 7,043 records and 21 variables. It includes customer demographics, contracted services, contract type, payment method, and monthly and total charges.

One thing worth flagging from the analysis: TotalCharges is largely the result of multiplying tenure by MonthlyCharges, which introduces some multicollinearity. Not a critical issue, but something to keep in mind.

---

## The Model

Four algorithms were tested: Logistic Regression, Random Forest, AdaBoost, and Gradient Boosting. With the default threshold of 0.50, Gradient Boosting was the clear winner on recall (0.82). But the default threshold makes no sense in this context.

By lowering the threshold to 0.30, Logistic Regression reaches a recall of 0.927 — virtually identical to the best-performing model — but with one major advantage: it is fully interpretable. You can look at the coefficients and know exactly what is driving each prediction. With Gradient Boosting, you do not have that.

The model was validated using GridSearchCV across 25 combinations and 5 folds. The optimal parameter turned out to be C=1, which is the default value. That is not a failure — it is a confirmation that the base model was already well calibrated.

---

## Key Findings

The monthly contract is the clearest risk factor. Customers on a month-to-month contract churn at a significantly higher rate than those on annual or two-year contracts. It makes sense: less commitment means it is easier to leave.

Fiber optic customers also show higher churn than DSL customers. This might seem counterintuitive given it is the premium service, but it likely reflects stronger competition in that segment and higher customer expectations.

New customers with less than 12 months of tenure are the most vulnerable. After that first year, churn probability drops considerably.

The electronic check payment method also correlates with higher churn, possibly indicating a customer profile that is less committed to automated billing.

---

## Results

With the threshold optimized to 0.30, the model detects 521 out of 561 real churners in the dataset. The AUC-ROC score is 0.844, indicating strong discriminative ability.

In business terms, if those predictions are acted upon with a retention campaign — assuming 200 euros recovered per retained customer and 5 euros per call — the estimated net benefit is around 101,800 euros per retention cycle. Compared to the 0 euros generated by doing nothing, the difference speaks for itself.

---

## Conclusions

The most important decision in this project was not technical — it was a business decision: understanding that optimizing the threshold is more valuable than optimizing the algorithm. Simply adjusting that one parameter took us from detecting 236 churners to 521.

The second conclusion is that interpretability has real value. A model you can explain to a marketing team or a commercial director is far more useful than one with better metrics that nobody understands.

And the third is that GridSearchCV confirming the default value is not bad news. It is information: the model did not need more complexity.

---

## Areas for Improvement

The dataset is static and from 2017. A production model would need updated data and a periodic retraining process to stay relevant over time.

There are no customer satisfaction variables. NPS scores, support tickets, technical response times — all of that would likely improve predictive power significantly.

The model treats all churn cases the same, but a customer leaving for a better offer is not the same as one leaving after a bad experience. Distinguishing between voluntary and involuntary churn would allow for much more targeted retention strategies.

Finally, the class imbalance (73.5% vs 26.5%) was managed through threshold adjustment, but techniques like SMOTE or class_weight balancing could be explored to improve the base performance before threshold optimization.
